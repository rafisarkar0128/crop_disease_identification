# Jute

**Jute** _(Corchorus olitorius / Corchorus capsularis)_, known as the "Golden Fibre" of Bangladesh, is a long, soft, shiny vegetable fiber produced from the Corchorus genus, primarily White jute (_Corchorus capsularis_) and Tossa jute (_Corchorus olitorius_). It is a ligno-cellulosic bast fiber composed mainly of cellulose and lignin, renowned for being biodegradable, eco-friendly, and a vital cash crop that supports the livelihoods of approximately 25 million people.

Jute _(Corchorus spp., family Malvaceae)_ is the golden thread woven into the identity of Bangladesh — a fiber crop so deeply embedded in the nation's economy and culture that it earned the title "Golden Fibre" long before independence. Grown across the floodplains of the Ganges–Brahmaputra delta, Bangladesh accounts for a dominant share of global jute production, with the crop cultivated primarily during the Kharif season (March–August). The Bangladesh Jute Research Institute (BJRI) has developed dozens of high-yielding varieties suited to the country's diverse agroclimatic zones, helping smallholder farmers maintain productivity in an era of climate uncertainty. Jute fiber is used in sacking, burlap, ropes, carpets, geotextiles, and increasingly in composite materials and eco-packaging — a renaissance driven by the global push away from synthetic plastics. But beyond commerce, jute is identity: the rustle of jute sacks at village markets, the smell of retting pools in August, the backbreaking harvest that generations of Bengali farmers have known. Bangladesh without jute is almost unimaginable — it is the crop that built the mills of Narayanganj and, for a century, clothed the world's goods.

# Jute (_Corchorus_ spp.) — Diseases, Symptoms & Cure

> Jute is a critical fiber crop of Bangladesh and South Asia. This document covers all major fungal, viral, and nematode diseases of jute, with pathogens, symptoms, and management strategies for each.
