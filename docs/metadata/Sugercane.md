# Sugarcane

**Sugarcane** _(Saccharum officinarum)_, known as the "White Gold" of tropical agriculture, is a tall perennial grass belonging to the family Poaceae, cultivated primarily for its high sucrose content stored in the fibrous stalks. It is the world's largest crop by production quantity and serves as the primary source of sugar, bioethanol, and molasses — making it a vital commercial and industrial crop that supports the livelihoods of hundreds of millions of farmers and workers globally.

Sugarcane _(Saccharum spp., family Poaceae)_ is one of the most economically significant crops in the world — a towering grass whose thick, juice-laden stalks have fueled civilizations, trade empires, and modern industries alike. Grown across tropical and subtropical regions between latitudes 35°N and 35°S, sugarcane thrives in warm climates with abundant rainfall and well-drained soils. Bangladesh cultivates sugarcane primarily in the northern districts — Rajshahi, Natore, Pabna, and Rangpur — where the crop forms an essential part of the agricultural economy. The Bangladesh Sugarcane Research Institute (BSRI) has developed numerous high-yielding, disease-resistant varieties adapted to local agroclimatic conditions. Beyond raw sugar production, sugarcane is the foundation of molasses, rum, bioethanol, and bagasse-based products like paper and particleboard — placing it at the intersection of food, energy, and industry. The harvest season, marked by the rhythmic swing of machetes and the sweet, heavy scent of cut cane drifting across the fields, is a defining moment in the agricultural calendar of sugarcane-growing regions. Sugarcane is not merely a crop — it is an ecosystem, an economy, and a way of life for millions who depend on its annual cycle of growth, harvest, and renewal.

# Sugarcane (_Saccharum_ spp.) — Diseases, Symptoms & Cure

> Sugarcane is a critical commercial crop of Bangladesh and tropical Asia. This document covers all major fungal, viral, and nematode diseases of sugarcane, with pathogens, symptoms, and management strategies for each.

## Possible Diseases of Sugarcane

### Fungal Diseases

| Disease | Pathogen | Symptoms | Treatment / Management | Image |
| --------------- | ------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Red Rot | _Colletotrichum falcatum_ | Reddening of internal stalk tissue with white patches; leaves turn yellow and dry from the tip; infected stalks emit a sour alcoholic smell; one of the most destructive diseases of sugarcane causing major yield and quality loss | Treat setts with Carbendazim (0.1%) or hot water treatment (50°C for 2 hours) before planting; use disease-free, certified seed cane; grow resistant varieties; rogue out and destroy infected clumps immediately; avoid ratoon cropping from infected fields; apply balanced fertilization | <img src="" width="200"> |
| Smut | _Sporisorium scitamineum_ | A whip-like black structure emerges from the growing tip of infected shoots, replacing the normal shoot; the whip is covered with black powdery spores; plant becomes stunted and unproductive; spreads rapidly through wind-dispersed spores | Use smut-free certified planting material; treat setts with hot water (50°C for 30 minutes) or Carbendazim before planting; destroy smutted whips immediately by burning before spores disperse; grow BSRI-recommended resistant varieties; avoid planting in fields with history of smut | <img src="" width="200"> |
| Wilt | _Fusarium sacchari_ | Stalks show shredding of internal tissues with pink-to-red discoloration; leaves turn yellow and wilt progressively; plant growth is stunted; infected stalks become hollow and lightweight; root development is poor | Use disease-free seed cane; treat setts with Carbendazim (0.1%) before planting; improve field drainage to avoid waterlogging; practice crop rotation; remove and burn infected plant material; apply Trichoderma-based biocontrol to the soil at planting | <img src="" width="200"> |
| Pokkah Boeng | _Fusarium moniliforme_ | Young leaves emerge twisted, crumpled, and distorted with irregular chlorotic patches; in severe cases, the top of the stalk rots and collapses ("top rot" phase); knife-cut lesions appear on the leaf midrib; more prevalent during the rainy season | Spray Carbendazim (0.1%) or Mancozeb (0.25%) at early symptom appearance; avoid excess nitrogen application; remove and destroy severely infected growing tips; ensure balanced K and Zn nutrition; use resistant varieties recommended by BSRI | <img src="" width="200"> |
| Leaf Scald | _Xanthomonas albilineans_ | Pencil-thin white or pale yellow stripes run along the length of the leaf blade parallel to the midrib; in chronic phase, leaves are chlorotic and plant growth is severely stunted; in acute phase, shoots wilt and die suddenly | Use certified disease-free seed cane; hot water treat setts at 50°C for 30 minutes; rogue out and destroy infected plants; strictly avoid using planting material from infected fields; sterilize cutting tools between plants; grow resistant varieties | <img src="" width="200"> |
| Brown Spot | _Cercospora longipes_ | Small yellowish spots appear on leaves that enlarge into brown oval lesions with yellow halos; lesions may coalesce, covering large areas of the leaf blade; reduces photosynthesis and overall plant vigor; more prevalent in humid conditions | Spray Mancozeb 75 WP (2.5 g/L) or Zineb (0.2%) at first symptom appearance; remove heavily infected leaves; improve field drainage; avoid excess nitrogen; maintain balanced fertilization; repeat spray at 14-day intervals if disease pressure is high | <img src="" width="200"> |
| Pineapple Disease | _Ceratocystis paradoxa_ | Freshly cut setts develop a pineapple-like fruity odor due to fungal colonization; internal tissue turns red then black; germination of planted setts is severely reduced; entire sett may rot before emergence | Treat setts with Carbendazim (0.1%) or Thiram (0.3%) immediately after cutting; plant fresh setts promptly without prolonged storage; avoid planting in waterlogged or poorly drained soils; use healthy planting material from disease-free nurseries | <img src="" width="200"> |
| Ring Spot | _Leptosphaeria sacchari_ | Circular to oval dark brown spots with reddish-purple borders form on leaves; spots may have a straw-colored center giving a ring-like appearance; heavy infection causes premature leaf drying; crop quality and sucrose content may be reduced | Apply Mancozeb (0.25%) or Copper oxychloride (3 g/L) sprays at early disease onset; remove and destroy infected leaf trash; maintain proper plant spacing for good air circulation; avoid excess moisture on foliage; use resistant varieties | <img src="" width="200"> |

### Viral and Vector-Borne Diseases

| Disease | Pathogen / Vector | Symptoms | Treatment / Management | Image |
| ------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Sugarcane Mosaic | Sugarcane Mosaic Virus (SCMV); vector: aphids (_Melanaphis sacchari_, _Rhopalosiphum maidis_) | Pale green and yellow mosaic streaks and blotches on young leaves; leaves may be narrower and shorter than normal; plant growth is stunted and sucrose yield is reduced; symptoms more prominent on young emerging leaves | Control aphid vectors with Imidacloprid 17.8 SL (0.5 mL/L) or Thiamethoxam spray; use certified virus-free seed cane; rogue out and destroy infected plants immediately; avoid planting near maize or sorghum which may harbor the virus; use resistant or tolerant varieties | <img src="" width="200"> |
| Grassy Shoot Disease | Sugarcane Grassy Shoot Phytoplasma; vector: leafhopper (_Pyrilla perpusilla_) | Infected shoots are thin, pale yellow, and numerous — giving a grass-like bushy appearance to the plant; leaves are narrow, short, and chlorotic; no usable stalk is produced; severely affected plants are entirely unproductive | Use certified disease-free planting material; hot water treat setts at 50°C for 30 minutes; control leafhopper vectors with Imidacloprid or Malathion spray; rogue out and burn all infected clumps; avoid ratoon cropping from infected stools; maintain weed-free fields to reduce vector habitats | <img src="" width="200"> |
