# Rice

**Rice** _(Oryza sativa)_, culturally and economically important grass and its edible starchy cereal grain. Roughly one-half of the world population, including virtually all of East and Southeast Asia, is wholly dependent upon rice as a staple food; 95 percent of the world's rice crop is eaten by humans. Rice is cooked by boiling, or it can be ground into a flour. It is eaten alone and in a great variety of soups, side dishes, and main dishes in Asian, Middle Eastern, and many other cuisines.

Rice _(Oryza sativa, family Poaceae)_ is the lifeblood of Bangladesh — not just a crop, but the quiet force that has fed, shaped, and held together an entire civilization. Sitting upon the impossibly fertile delta of the Ganges and Brahmaputra rivers, Bangladesh ranks among the world's top four rice producers, harvesting it across three seasons: Aus (summer), Aman (monsoon), and Boro (winter) — each one a rhythm the people here have known for thousands of years. With an average consumption of ~180kg per person annually, bhat — plain, steamed rice — is not just food, it is what a meal fundamentally is, served alongside dal, fish curry, and vegetables in a daily ritual unchanged for generations. The Bangladesh Rice Research Institute (BRRI) has spent decades performing quiet miracles, developing over 100 high-yield, flood and drought-resistant varieties that transformed a once food-insecure nation into a near self-sufficient one, now feeding over 170 million people. But beyond the science and the fields, rice runs in the cultural bloodstream of Bangladesh — central to the harvest festival of Nabanna, scattered at weddings, and alive in the most tender everyday question one Bengali asks another: "Bhat kheyecho?" — have you had rice? In just three words, a whole civilization's worth of love.
