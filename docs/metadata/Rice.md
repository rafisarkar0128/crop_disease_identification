# Rice

**Rice** _(Oryza sativa)_, culturally and economically important grass and its edible starchy cereal grain. Roughly one-half of the world population, including virtually all of East and Southeast Asia, is wholly dependent upon rice as a staple food; 95 percent of the world's rice crop is eaten by humans. Rice is cooked by boiling, or it can be ground into a flour. It is eaten alone and in a great variety of soups, side dishes, and main dishes in Asian, Middle Eastern, and many other cuisines.

Rice _(Oryza sativa, family Poaceae)_ is the lifeblood of Bangladesh — not just a crop, but the quiet force that has fed, shaped, and held together an entire civilization. Sitting upon the impossibly fertile delta of the Ganges and Brahmaputra rivers, Bangladesh ranks among the world's top four rice producers, harvesting it across three seasons: Aus (summer), Aman (monsoon), and Boro (winter) — each one a rhythm the people here have known for thousands of years. With an average consumption of ~180kg per person annually, bhat — plain, steamed rice — is not just food, it is what a meal fundamentally is, served alongside dal, fish curry, and vegetables in a daily ritual unchanged for generations. The Bangladesh Rice Research Institute (BRRI) has spent decades performing quiet miracles, developing over 100 high-yield, flood and drought-resistant varieties that transformed a once food-insecure nation into a near self-sufficient one, now feeding over 170 million people. But beyond the science and the fields, rice runs in the cultural bloodstream of Bangladesh — central to the harvest festival of Nabanna, scattered at weddings, and alive in the most tender everyday question one Bengali asks another: "Bhat kheyecho?" — have you had rice? In just three words, a whole civilization's worth of love.

# Rice (_Oryza sativa_) — Diseases, Symptoms & Cure.

> Rice is the lifeblood of Bangladesh and much of Asia. This document covers all known bacterial, fungal, viral, and miscellaneous diseases of rice, with symptoms for each.

| **Rice black-streaked dwarf virus**                    | Stunting and dark green discoloration; white to pale waxy enations (galls) on the underside of leaves and on leaf sheaths; veins on the lower leaf surface appear swollen and distorted; panicle partially enclosed in the flag leaf sheath; transmitted by planthoppers | No cure once infected. **Prevention:** Control planthopper vectors using insecticides (imidacloprid, thiamethoxam); use resistant or tolerant varieties; remove and destroy infected plants early; avoid late planting during peak vector populations; maintain field sanitation.                                           |

| **Rice stripe necrosis virus**                         | Yellow to pale green stripes along the leaf veins; necrotic streaking; young leaves may be killed; stunting; transmitted by _Polymyxa graminis_ in soil                                                                                                                  | No cure once infected. **Prevention:** Improve field drainage; practice rotation with non-cereal crops; apply calcium or lime to reduce soil-borne vector activity; use tolerant varieties; avoid continuous rice monoculture.                                                                                              |
| **Rice stripe tenuivirus**                             | Pale yellow to white stripes along the veins of young leaves; new leaves may be entirely white or chlorotic; affected plants fail to produce panicles or produce sterile ones; older leaves may show rolling and wilting; transmitted by the small brown planthopper     | No cure once infected. **Prevention:** Control small brown planthopper with insecticides; use resistant varieties; early planting to escape peak vector flights; remove infected plants and weed hosts; use sticky traps or light traps for vector monitoring.                                                              |
| **Rice transitory yellowing virus**                    | Yellowing of younger leaves, particularly the upper leaves; symptoms are transitory and may disappear as the plant matures; mild stunting; transmitted by leafhoppers in East Asia                                                                                       | No cure once infected. **Prevention:** Apply insecticides to control leafhopper vectors; symptoms may naturally subside — avoid removing plants prematurely; use resistant or tolerant varieties; practice good field hygiene and remove heavily infected plants early.                                                     |
| **Rice tungro bacilliform virus** _(see Tungro below)_ | Component of the tungro complex: yellow-orange leaf discoloration beginning from the leaf tip; stunting; reduced tillering; delayed heading; incomplete panicle exsertion and sterile grains                                                                             | No cure once infected. **Prevention:** See _Rice tungro_ under Miscellaneous Diseases below.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                |
| **Rice tungro spherical virus** _(see Tungro below)_   | The helper virus of the tungro complex; in combination with RTBV causes classic tungro: yellow-orange discoloration, stunting, and sterility; transmitted by the green leafhopper _Nephotettix_ spp.                                                                     | No cure once infected. **Prevention:** See _Rice tungro_ under Miscellaneous Diseases below.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                |
| **Rice yellow mottle virus**                           | Bright yellow mottling, streaking, and mosaic on leaves; stunting; plant may turn entirely yellow-orange; reduced tillering; sterile or empty panicles; transmitted by insects and mechanically; major constraint in sub-Saharan Africa                                  | No cure once infected. **Prevention:** Use resistant varieties; avoid mechanical transmission by sanitizing farm tools with 10% bleach solution; control insect vectors; remove infected plants immediately; use certified clean seed; avoid movement of infected plant material between fields.                            |

## Miscellaneous Diseases and Disorders

| Disease / Disorder               | Cause                                                                                                                            | Symptoms                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                 | Management / Cure                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                         |
| -------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Alkalinity or salt damage**    | Excessive salt concentration in soil or water                                                                                    | Leaf tips turn brown and die back; leaf margins show white to brownish scorching; stunting; reduced tillering; roots turn dark and rot; white salt crust may appear on soil surface                                                                                      | **Management:** Apply gypsum (calcium sulfate) to displace sodium from soil; leach salts by flooding and draining fields repeatedly; apply organic matter (compost, green manure) to improve soil structure; use salt-tolerant varieties; maintain adequate water supply to dilute salt concentration; apply zinc sulfate (25 kg/ha) if zinc deficiency accompanies salt stress.                                                                                                          |
| **Bronzing**                     | Zinc deficiency                                                                                                                  | Small, irregular, reddish-brown spots on older leaves that coalesce into bronzy discoloration; leaves may turn entirely brown and die; stunting; roots turn dark brown; most common in waterlogged, organic soils                                                        | **Management:** Apply zinc sulfate (ZnSO₄) at 25–50 kg/ha as basal application or foliar spray (0.5% ZnSO₄ solution); drain field periodically to allow soil aeration; avoid excessive phosphorus application which inhibits zinc uptake; use zinc-efficient varieties; apply zinc-enriched fertilizers during nursery stage.                                                                                                                                                             |
| **Cold injury**                  | Low temperatures                                                                                                                 | Delayed germination and seedling emergence; whitish or pale yellow discoloration of young leaves; poor tillering; distorted or white (albino) panicles; high spikelet sterility especially during booting and heading                                                    | **Management:** Use cold-tolerant varieties suited to the region; adjust planting date to avoid cold-sensitive growth stages coinciding with low temperatures; maintain deep water (10–15 cm) in the field during cold spells to insulate root zone; apply potassium fertilizers (K₂O) to improve cold tolerance; use protective structures for seedling nurseries.                                                                                                                       |
| **Panicle blight**               | Cause undetermined                                                                                                               | Blighting and sterility of spikelets on the panicle; grain filling is poor or absent; affected spikelets are often discolored; may be confused with bacterial or fungal diseases                                                                                         | **Management:** Cause not fully established — general measures include: avoid water stress during panicle development; maintain balanced fertilization (avoid excess nitrogen); apply fungicides (propiconazole, tebuconazole) as a precaution if fungal involvement is suspected; improve field drainage; use well-adapted varieties with good panicle exsertion.                                                                                                                        |
| **Golden apple snail**           | _Pomacea canaliculata_                                                                                                           | Irregular, ragged destruction of young seedlings at or below the waterline; leaves and stems chewed off; plants completely consumed in severe cases; pink egg masses visible on plant surfaces and field structures above the waterline                                  | **Management:** Manually collect and crush snails and egg masses; lower water level during early crop establishment (0–2 cm) to reduce feeding zones; install 1 cm mesh screens at water inlets; apply molluscicides (metaldehyde, niclosamide) at recommended rates; introduce natural predators (ducks, carp); use older (>21-day) seedlings for transplanting which are more resistant; scatter crushed shells or lime to deter snails.                                                |
| **Pecky rice (kernel spotting)** | Feeding injury by rice stink bug, _Oebalus pugnax_                                                                               | Small, dark discolored spots on grain caused by stylet-feeding punctures; endosperm shows chalky or necrotic spots; affected grains are of poor milling quality; damage occurs during the milky to dough stage                                                           | **Management:** Apply insecticides (lambda-cyhalothrin, malathion) during the milky stage when stink bugs are most damaging; time insecticide applications at early morning when bugs are most active; maintain weed-free field margins to reduce stink bug refugia; use pheromone traps for monitoring; avoid late-season planting that coincides with stink bug peaks.                                                                                                                  |
| **Rice tungro**                  | Complex virus (Rice tungro bacilliform virus and Rice tungro spherical virus) transmitted by green leafhopper _Nephotettix_ spp. | Yellowing and orange discoloration of leaves beginning at the tip and spreading to the base; stunting proportional to infection age; severely stunted plants produce no panicles; older infections may show mottling; conspicuous yellow patches appear across the field | **Management:** No cure once infected. **Prevention:** Use tungro-resistant varieties (IR64, IR72, IRRI-developed varieties with _Glh_ resistance); apply systemic insecticides (imidacloprid, thiamethoxam) to control green leafhopper vector at transplanting; rogue out and destroy infected plants early; synchronize planting with neighboring farms to break the disease cycle; avoid planting adjacent to old, infected crops; use light traps to monitor leafhopper populations. |
| **Straighthead**                 | Arsenic-induced, unknown physiological disorder                                                                                  | Empty, sterile spikelets that remain upright ("straight") instead of bending with grain weight; glumes may show dark discoloration; leaves may have "parrot beak" distortion; hull may have dark blotches; roots are often dark and shallow                              | **Management:** Drain and dry the field (mid-season drainage) for 7–10 days before and after flooding to reduce arsenic availability in soil; avoid fields with a history of straighthead; use straighthead-resistant varieties; apply silica (silicon) fertilizers to compete with arsenic uptake; avoid organic soils with high arsenic content; use clean irrigation water free from arsenic contamination.                                                                            |
| **White tip** _(see nematodes)_  | _Aphelenchoides besseyi_                                                                                                         | White or pale discoloration and necrosis at the tips of young leaves, which later die back; leaf tips may twist and become crinkled; flag leaf may be partially white; spikelets are sterile or partially filled; nematode is seed-borne and also infests the panicle    | **Management:** Use certified nematode-free seed; treat seed with hot water at 52°C for 10–15 minutes before sowing (effective against seed-borne nematodes); apply carbofuran (3G) at transplanting; avoid using seed from infected crops; practice crop rotation with non-host crops; apply nematicides to seedling nurseries in heavily infested fields; use resistant varieties where available.                                                                                      |

## Insect Pests of Rice

**B**

- Brown planthopper — Hopperburn: circular yellowing and browning patches; plants wilt and dry completely; also vectors grassy stunt and ragged stunt viruses

**C**

- _Chilo suppressalis_ — "Deadheart" (wilted central shoot) during vegetative stage; "whitehead" (dead, empty panicle) at reproductive stage; circular feeding holes on leaves; frass inside the stem
- _Cletus punctiger_ — Discolored, shriveled, or deformed grains from sucking during milky stage; "pecky rice" grain discoloration
- _Cnaphalocrocis medinalis_ — Leaves folded longitudinally and tied with silk; green tissue scraped leaving white, papery streaks; folded leaf tubes visible on the crop canopy

**D**

- _Dicladispa armigera_ — Irregular white to brown blotches caused by larvae mining inside the leaf; adults scrape the upper leaf surface leaving white streaks; severe infestation causes leaves to dry up
- _Diopsis_ — Deadheart symptoms in young plants; larvae bore into the stem at the base

**H**

- _Hydrellia philippina_ — Ragged, elongated holes on young leaves; transparent windows or scratches on leaf surface; young leaves fail to unfurl properly in severe cases
- _Hysteroneura_ — Stunting; leaf curling and yellowing; honeydew secretions promote sooty mold growth; potential virus vector

**L**

- _Laodelphax striatellus_ — Direct feeding causes yellowing; major vector of rice stripe tenuivirus; hopperburn in dense infestations
- _Leptocorisa acuta_ — Sucking damage to filling grains; shriveling, discoloration, and unfilled grains; characteristic pungent odor in the field
- _Leptocorisa ayamaruensis_ — Grain sucking causing shriveled and discolored kernels; pungent odor
- _Leptocorisa biguttata_ — Grain sucking causing shriveled and discolored kernels
- _Leptocorisa bipunctata_ — Grain sucking damage; reduced grain fill and discoloration
- _Leptocorisa chinensis_ — Sucking damage to developing grains; shriveling and discoloration
- _Leptocorisa costalis_ — Grain sucking; shriveled and empty spikelets; "pecky rice"
- _Leptocorisa discoidalis_ — Grain sucking damage; reduced grain quality
- _Leptocorisa lepida_ — Sucking of developing grains; discoloration and shriveling
- _Leptocorisa luzonensis_ — Grain sucking damage; reduced yield and grain quality
- _Leptocorisa luzonica_ — Grain sucking; shriveled and discolored kernels
- _Leptocorisa oratoria_ — Sucking damage during grain filling; shriveling; pungent field odor
- _Leptocorisa palawanensis_ — Grain sucking damage; discolored and shriveled grains
- _Leptocorisa pseudolepida_ — Sucking of developing grains; discoloration and shriveling
- _Leptocorisa sakdapolrakae_ — Grain sucking damage; reduced grain fill
- _Leptocorisa solomonensis_ — Sucking damage to developing spikelets; grain discoloration
- _Leptocorisa tagalica_ — Grain sucking; shriveled and discolored kernels
- _Leptocorisa timorensis_ — Sucking damage during grain filling; shriveling and discoloration

**M**

- _Maiestas dorsalis_ — Yellowing and stunting from direct feeding; vector of tungro and other viruses
- _Maliarpha separatella_ — Deadheart and whitehead symptoms; larvae bore into the stem; transparent windows on leaves from early-instar feeding
- _Mythimna separata_ — Defoliation with large, ragged leaf feeding; in outbreak conditions entire fields can be stripped; young larvae cause "windowing" on leaves

**N**

- _Nephotettix_ — Yellowing from direct feeding; critical vector of rice tungro virus and rice dwarf virus
- _Nymphula depunctalis_ — Larvae cut leaf pieces to form tubular cases; feeding causes transparent "windows" on leaves; damaged leaves roll and float on water

**O**

- _Oebalus pugnax_ — Pecky rice: feeding punctures on developing grain cause chalky, discolored kernels; reduces milling yield and market value
- _Orseolia_ — Silver shoot or "onion leaf": infested central tiller becomes a tubular, silvery-white gall; tiller produces no panicle
- _Orseolia oryzae_ (Asian rice gall midge) — Silver shoot: infested tiller converted to a hollow, silvery-white tubular gall resembling an onion leaf; no panicle produced from affected tillers
- _Orseolia oryzivora_ (African rice gall midge) — Similar to Asian gall midge; central tiller converted into a characteristic tubular, silvery gall; no panicle produced
- _Oxya_ — Defoliation: irregular notching and chewing of leaf margins; heavy infestations cause significant leaf area loss
- _Oxya chinensis_ — Chewing damage to leaf margins and blades; defoliation in high densities
- _Oxya hyla_ — Leaf margin notching and chewing; defoliation in outbreak conditions

**P**

- _Parapoynx stagnalis_ — Larvae feed on submerged leaf tissue; "windowpane" effect on submerged leaves; young seedlings may be destroyed in flooded nurseries

**R**

- _Recilia dorsalis_ — Yellowing from direct feeding; vector of rice tungro and other viruses
- Rice bug — Sucking of filling grains: shriveling, discoloration, and empty grains; strong pungent odor in the field
- Rice weevil — Storage pest: small, round holes bored into grain; internal feeding leaves only the hull; grain dust and weight loss in storage

**S**

- _Scirpophaga incertulas_ (yellow stem borer) — Deadheart in vegetative stage; whitehead at reproductive stage; larvae bore into stems; frass and feeding tunnels inside cut stems
- _Scirpophaga innotata_ (white stem borer) — Same as yellow stem borer: deadheart and whitehead; frass and feeding tunnels in stems
- _Scotinophara_ — Circular yellowing patches ("bugburn"); leaves turn yellow to orange-brown; toxic saliva injected during feeding; severe infestations cause complete drying of the crop
- _Sesamia inferens_ (pink stem borer) — Deadheart and whitehead; larvae bore through the side of stems; frass visible at entry holes
- _Sogatella furcifera_ (whitebacked planthopper) — Hopperburn: yellowing and drying of plants at high densities; vector of southern rice black-streaked dwarf virus
- _Spodoptera litura_ (armyworm) — Ragged defoliation; young larvae feed in groups causing "windowpane" effect; older larvae cause major leaf loss; nocturnal feeding
- _Stenchaetothrips biformis_ (rice thrips) — Silvery, papery streaking and rolling of young leaf blades; tips of affected leaves may wither and die; seedlings show distorted, curled leaves
- _Stenocoris_ — Grain sucking damage; shriveled and discolored kernels; similar to other rice bug species

**T**

- _Tryporhyza_ — Deadheart and whitehead; entry holes on stems; frass and boring tunnels inside the culm; characteristic stem borer damage pattern
